    Honors Thesis: The Impact of Mindfulness Training on Working Memory Performance and White Matter Microstructural Integrity in Major White Matter Tracts Associated With the Hippocampus
    In collaboration with the Lazar Lab at Havard University, I Investigated whether mindfulness training (MT) modulates hippocampal white matter microstructural integrity (WMMI) and working memory performance in aging adults. Using diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) and behavioral measures, I found that MT was associated with increased white matter integrity in major hippocampal tracts (cingulum bundle and uncinate fasciculus) and improvements in working memory performance. Notably, gains in working memory were linked to increases in left dorsal cingulum bundle integrity. This work contributed to understanding how contemplative training may support cognitive aging through structural brain plasticity. Awarded the CLPS Premium for Excellence in the Cognitive Neuroscience Concentration.

    Capstone Project: The Role of Mindfulness Meditation in Modulating Cognitive Performance, Anxiety, and Fear-Related Behavior
    This capstone project used a multimodal approach to examine how mindfulness meditation may influence cognition and emotional regulation. I conducted an extensive literature review alongside independent analyses of neuroimaging data, focusing on white matter microstructure within amygdala-associated tracts and its relationship to anxiety measures. Although quantitative analyses did not reveal significant associations, the project also incorporated qualitative interviews with long-term mindfulness practitioners and instructors. Integrating empirical data with lived experience deepened my understanding of mind–brain relationships and reinforced the value of combining quantitative and experiential methods in cognitive neuroscience research. Awarded Francisco Valera Prize for Outstanding Undergraduate Thesis.

Projects

Current projects

Aperiodic Neural Activity and Sleep Microarchitecture: Links to Spindles and K-Complexes

This project investigates whether aperiodic activity during N2 sleep is associated with the occurrence and detection of K-complexes and sleep spindles. Using EEG data, we isolate N2 sleep and fit spectral parameterization models to characterize aperiodic dynamics in relation to annotated sleep events. This links cortical excitation–inhibition balance to sleep microarchitecture, potentially clarifying variability in event detection and advancing our understanding of sleep-dependent memory consolidation.

repo

Aperiodic Neural Activity as a Marker of Excitation/Inhibition Balance in Children with Tourette Syndrome

In collaboration with the Greene Developmental Cognitive Neuroscience Lab, this project investigates whether cortical EI balance- indexed by aperiodic activity- changes in adolescents with Tourette Syndrome (TS) following Comprehensive Behavioral Intervention for Tics (CBIT). Spectral parameterization is applied to recordings pre- and post-treatment to examine whether tic improvement corresponds to more balanced EI dynamics. By integrating behavioral intervention with neural biomarkers, this work aims to clarify the physiological mechanisms underlying tic reduction and support the development of objective markers of treatment response.

repo

Biophysical Neural Modeling with the Human Neocortical Neurosolver to Uncover Multiscale Thalamocortical Dynamics in Visually-Evoked Potentials

In collaboration with the Jones Computational Neuroscience Lab at Brown University, this study uses the Human Neocortical Neurosolver (HNN) to model the cellular and circuit mechanisms underlying visually evoked ERPs in human primary visual cortex (V1). By simulating layer-specific thalamocortical and corticocortical inputs, the model reproduces source-localized V1 ERP components and links macroscopic EEG signals to underlying microcircuit dynamics. Preliminary findings suggest a canonical thalamocortical sequence supporting early visual processing and provide multiscale predictions consistent with invasive animal studies.
